# Axis 1 — Missing Growth-Unlocking Integration

> Prospecting scoring axis for **Carl Maloney**, fractional CTO selling integration / technical leadership.
> **Question this axis answers:** Does this company likely DEPEND on a key third-party system-of-record (SoR) AND would it unlock material business growth by building/owning an integration to it that it does not yet have (or has only a weak version of)?
>
> A high score = the company sells INTO an ecosystem anchored by a big SoR, OR its customers/operations require connecting TO one, AND there is evidence the integration is missing, partial, manual, or a deal-blocker. That is exactly the work Carl gets hired to lead.

## 3. Scoring Rubric — Axis 1 (0–5)

| Score | Anchor | What it looks like |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | No SoR dependence | Company does not sell into or depend on any major SoR. No integration language. Pure offline/services biz, or already a SoR itself with mature ecosystem and no gap. |
| **1** | Generic integration mention, no anchor SoR | Says "we integrate with your tools" / "open API" but names no major SoR and shows no ecosystem dependence. Weak, speculative. |
| **2** | Dependence implied by industry, no integration evidence | Industry clearly orbits a SoR (e.g., a digital-health point solution → Epic; a payroll-adjacent fintech → ADP) but the company shows no current integration and no stated gap. Latent need. |
| **3** | Names a SoR / ecosystem and shows it matters, but integration is partial/manual/weak | Mentions Epic/Salesforce/NetSuite/etc.; relies on CSV import, manual upload, SFTP, or "limited integrations." Clear that owning a real integration would help. |
| **4** | Clear ecosystem dependence + explicit growth gap signal | Marketplace presence with thin coverage, "request an integration" / roadmap / "coming soon" pages, OR a competitor has the integration and they don't (parity gap), OR integration is named as an enterprise-deal blocker. |
| **5** | Existential dependence on a SoR + the integration is the growth unlock | Their entire GTM is "sell into [Epic/Workday/Salesforce/Veeva/SAP] ecosystem," customers can't buy without the connection, and evidence shows it's missing, manual, or a bottleneck (e.g., "Epic integration coming 2026," EHR interop is the moat, claims/EDI flow is the product). Building/owning this integration directly unlocks revenue. |

**Scoring guidance**
- Anchor on the **strongest single SoR signal**, then add +1 if a "missing/wanted integration" gap signal is also present (cap at 5).
- EHR (Epic/athena/Cerner/Veradigm), ERP (SAP/NetSuite/Workday), Veeva, Salesforce, and HL7/FHIR + X12/EDI signals are the **highest-value anchors** — they map to large, hard, deal-gating integrations that fractional CTOs are hired to own.
- Stripe/Plaid/QuickBooks/Twilio alone tend to be **commodity** integrations (cap at 3 unless paired with a clear gap or enterprise-deal blocker).
- A company that **is** the SoR (e.g., Epic itself) scores 0 on this axis — it's the platform, not the dependent.

## 4. False Positives / Caveats

- **Already-mature integration ≠ opportunity.** A robust "integrates with Epic, Salesforce, and Workday" line may mean the work is DONE. Look for gap signals (roadmap, "coming soon," manual workarounds, thin marketplace footprint) before scoring 4–5.
- **Keyword surface ≠ dependence.** "Stripe" in a footer (just how they bill) or "Salesforce" as a logo on a customer list is not ecosystem dependence. Require the SoR to be tied to the company's value prop or customer workflow.
- **The vendor IS the SoR.** Epic, Salesforce, Workday, SAP, ServiceNow, Veeva, ADP themselves (and their direct resellers) are platforms, not prospects for this axis.
- **Pure integration vendors / iPaaS** (Workato, Tray, Merge, MuleSoft, Mirth, Redox, Boomi) sell integration as the product — they have the capability in-house; score low unless they're a *customer* needing a bespoke build.
- **Generic "open API" boilerplate** is marketing noise. Down-weight unless a specific SoR or gap is named.
- **Commodity payments/accounting** (Stripe, QuickBooks, Xero, Plaid, Twilio) are widely templated; high keyword frequency, low differentiation. Treat as supporting signal, not a primary anchor, unless an enterprise-deal blocker or parity gap is explicit.
- **Healthcare-interop double-counting.** Epic + HL7/FHIR + Carequality often co-occur; count them as ONE healthcare-interop anchor, not three, to avoid inflating scores.
- **Stale roadmap pages.** "Integration coming soon" can be years old (still a positive signal of intent, but verify recency before treating as a hot trigger).
- **EDI/X12 in logistics vs. healthcare** differ; segment by industry so a retail ASN (856) prospect isn't mistaken for a healthcare claims (837/835) prospect.
